Richard Feynman

“In that one sentence, you will see, there is an enormous amount of information about the world, if just a little imagination and thinking are applied.”

Known as the Great Explainer, Richard Feynman was famous for his easy-to-understand explanations of complex scientific concepts.

Here's a time line of Dr. Feynmann's life:

  • 1918 - born in Queens, New York City
  • 1939 - receives a bachelor's degree in the Massachusetts Institute of Technology
  • 1941 - spends the summer working on ballistics problems at the Frankford Arsenal in Pennsylvania
  • 1942 - receives a Ph.D. from Princeton
  • 1943 - departs for New Mexico, to the Los Alamos Laboratory, to work on desidning and building atomic bombs
  • 1945 - accepts Cornell's university offer and becomes one of the first of the Los Alamos Laboratory's group leaders to depart, leaving for Ithaca, New York
  • 1948 - publishes papers on "A Relativistic Cut-Off for Classical Electrodynamics"
  • 1965 - is awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics for successfully resolving problems related to the theory of quantum electrodynamics
  • 1972 - as a legendary educator, Feynman is awarded the Oersted Medal for Teaching
  • 1988 - dies at the age of 69
